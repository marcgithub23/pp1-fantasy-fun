Subgenres & Recommendations

High Fantasy

High fantasy, also known as epic fantasy, is a subgenre of fantasy literature that transports readers to entirely fictional worlds with complex histories, unique cultures, and intricate magic systems. It often features grand, sweeping narratives filled with heroes, villains, and epic quests to save the world. High fantasy worlds are meticulously crafted, and the stories within them explore timeless themes of good vs. evil, heroism, and the struggle for power. These tales are a departure from our reality, offering readers a chance to escape into realms of pure imagination.

These books showcase the rich storytelling, world-building, and character development that define the high fantasy subgenre:

The Lord of the Rings by J.R.R. Tolkien

A cornerstone of high fantasy, this trilogy follows the journey of Frodo Baggins to destroy the One Ring and save Middle-earth from the Dark Lord Sauron.

A Song of Ice and Fire series by George R.R. Martin

Beginning with "A Game of Thrones," this series is known for its sprawling cast of characters and political intrigue in the Seven Kingdoms of Westeros.

The Wheel of Time series by Robert Jordan

Starting with "The Eye of the World," this series spans 14 books and is an epic tale of a world teetering on the brink of an apocalyptic conflict.

Mistborn series by Brandon Sanderson

Beginning with "Mistborn: The Final Empire," this series features a unique magic system based on the consumption of metals and a young heroine's quest to overthrow a dark ruler.

The Name of the Wind by Patrick Rothfuss

The first book in the "Kingkiller Chronicle" series, it follows the life and adventures of Kvothe, a gifted musician, and wizard, as he recounts his story to a chronicler.

The Lies of Locke Lamora by Scott Lynch

The first book in the "Gentleman Bastard" series introduces Locke Lamora, a witty thief and con artist in the elaborate city of Camorr.

Low Fantasy

Low fantasy is a subgenre of fantasy literature characterised by its connection to the real world or a world that closely resembles our own. Unlike high fantasy, which typically features entirely fictional settings, low fantasy often introduces fantastical elements into a familiar, everyday environment. In low fantasy, the magical and supernatural exist subtly, and the focus is often on how ordinary people interact with or are affected by these elements. This subgenre blurs the lines between reality and fantasy, creating a sense of wonder in the ordinary.

These titles showcase the diversity of stories that can be told within the low fantasy subgenre, where magic and reality coexist in intriguing ways:

Harry Potter series by J.K. Rowling

Set in a magical world hidden within contemporary Britain, this series follows the young wizard Harry Potter as he navigates the challenges of both the wizarding and Muggle (non-magical) worlds.

Neverwhere by Neil Gaiman

In this novel, an ordinary man named Richard Mayhew stumbles into an alternate London, called London Below, where magic, danger, and strange creatures lurk beneath the city's surface.

The Dresden Files series by Jim Butcher

This low fantasy series follows Harry Dresden, a modern-day wizard and private investigator, as he takes on supernatural cases in Chicago.

Rivers of London series by Ben Aaronovitch

Beginning with "Rivers of London" (also known as "Midnight Riot" in the U.S.), this series features Peter Grant, a London police officer who becomes an apprentice wizard and investigates supernatural crimes.

The Magicians by Lev Grossman

This novel and its sequels introduce readers to Brakebills College for Magical Pedagogy, where students learn magic in a world that closely mirrors our own.

The Golem and the Jinni by Helene Wecker

Set in New York City at the turn of the 20th century, this novel explores the lives of a golem and a jinni who find themselves navigating the human world.

Grimdark Fantasy

Grimdark fantasy is a subgenre of fantasy literature known for its gritty, morally ambiguous, and often bleak portrayal of characters and settings. In grimdark, the lines between good and evil are blurred, and heroes may be flawed or morally compromised. The tone is usually dark, exploring themes of violence, power struggles, and the consequences of one's actions. It's a subgenre that delves into the harsh realities of a fictional world, often with a focus on complex characters and their struggles.

These titles showcase the dark, complex, and morally challenging nature of the grimdark fantasy subgenre, appealing to readers who enjoy stories that don't shy away from the harsh realities of their fictional worlds:

The Blade Itself by Joe Abercrombie

The first book in "The First Law" trilogy introduces readers to a world of political intrigue, war, and morally ambiguous characters, including the infamous Inquisitor Glokta.

The Broken Empire trilogy by Mark Lawrence

Beginning with "Prince of Thorns," this series follows the ruthless antihero Jorg Ancrath as he seeks revenge in a post-apocalyptic world.

The Black Company by Glen Cook

The first book in a long-running series, it follows the gritty adventures of a mercenary company in a world filled with war, sorcery, and morally ambiguous choices.

Malazan Book of the Fallen series by Steven Erikson

This epic series, starting with "Gardens of the Moon," is known for its sprawling scope, complex characters, and a world filled with gods, armies, and sorcery.

The Poppy War by R.F. Kuang

The first book in "The Poppy War" trilogy follows Rin, a war orphan who discovers her immense magical abilities and becomes entangled in a brutal conflict inspired by Chinese history.

The Library at Mount Char by Scott Hawkins

This standalone novel introduces readers to Carolyn, a former orphan who has been taken in by Father, a godlike figure with incredible powers. The story delves into dark, surreal realms as Carolyn and her fellow orphans seek to uncover the secrets of the universe, leading to a grim and enigmatic exploration of power, revenge, and morality in a fantastical setting.

Portal Fantasy

Portal fantasy is a subgenre of fantasy literature characterised by its premise of characters traveling from the ordinary, mundane world into a fantastical realm through a magical portal or doorway. These portals can take various forms, such as wardrobe doors, rabbit holes, or even ancient books. The protagonists typically experience adventures, face challenges, and confront magical elements in these new worlds, often transforming through their experiences.

These titles showcase the enchanting and imaginative nature of portal fantasy, where ordinary characters embark on extraordinary journeys through magical doorways:

The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe by C.S. Lewis

This classic novel introduces readers to Narnia, a magical land accessed through a wardrobe, where children embark on epic adventures.

Alice's Adventures in Wonderland by Lewis Carroll

Alice falls into a rabbit hole, leading her to the whimsical and absurd Wonderland, where she encounters peculiar characters and surreal situations.

Coraline by Neil Gaiman

In this eerie tale, Coraline discovers a hidden door in her home that leads to a parallel world with eerie doppelgangers and a menacing Other Mother.

Every Heart a Doorway by Seanan McGuire

This novella is set in a school for children who have returned from portal adventures and are struggling to adapt to the real world. It explores the aftermath of portal travel.

The Ten Thousand Doors of January by Alix E. Harrow

January Scaller discovers a book that tells of magical doors to other worlds, setting her on a quest to explore these portals and uncover family secrets.

The Subtle Knife by Philip Pullman

This is the second book in the "His Dark Materials" trilogy. It continues the adventures of Lyra Belacqua and introduces a new protagonist, Will Parry. The story explores multiple parallel universes connected by subtle, knife-like portals, adding a complex and fascinating dimension to the series.

Urban Fantasy

Urban fantasy is a subgenre of fantasy literature set in contemporary, often urban settings, where magical or supernatural elements exist alongside the everyday world. In these stories, characters grapple with magic, mythical creatures, or supernatural forces in modern cities, blurring the lines between the ordinary and the extraordinary. Urban fantasy explores how magic and fantasy can intersect with our familiar, urban landscapes, creating a unique blend of the mundane and the mystical.

These titles showcase the diverse and imaginative urban settings where magic and the supernatural coexist with the everyday in urban fantasy literature:

The Mortal Instruments series by Cassandra Clare

Beginning with "City of Bones," this series features a hidden world of demons, angels, and shadowhunters in contemporary New York City.

Mercy Thompson series by Patricia Briggs

This series follows Mercedes "Mercy" Thompson, a mechanic and coyote shape-shifter, as she navigates a world filled with werewolves, vampires, and fae.

The Hollows series by Kim Harrison

Beginning with "Dead Witch Walking," this series features Rachel Morgan, a witch and bounty hunter, in a world where supernatural beings live openly in Cincinnati.

The Iron Druid Chronicles series by Kevin Hearne

This series follows Atticus O'Sullivan, the last of the Druids, as he navigates the modern world, battles gods and mythical creatures, and protects the world from supernatural threats.

October Daye series by Seanan McGuire

Starting with "Rosemary and Rue," this series features October "Toby" Daye, a changeling private investigator, as she uncovers mysteries in the hidden world of Faerie while living in San Francisco.

The Kate Daniels series by Ilona Andrews

This series introduces Kate Daniels, a magic-wielding mercenary in a post-apocalyptic Atlanta where magic and technology ebb and flow, causing supernatural challenges.

Steampunk Fantasy

Steampunk fantasy is a subgenre of speculative fiction that combines elements of the Victorian era with advanced steam-powered technology and imaginative, often fantastical, inventions. It's characterised by its blend of historical settings, retro-futuristic technology, and a strong emphasis on aesthetics and fashion. Steampunk worlds are filled with airships, clockwork contraptions, and a sense of adventure reminiscent of the 19th century, creating a unique and visually rich subgenre.

These titles offer a diverse range of steampunk worlds and stories, showcasing the subgenre's fascination with blending history, technology, and fantasy:

Perdido Street Station by China Miéville

Set in the sprawling city of New Crobuzon, this novel introduces readers to a world filled with bizarre creatures, strange magic, and a host of steampunk inventions.

The Infernal Devices trilogy by Cassandra Clare

Set in a Victorian-era London where demon hunters operate secretly, this series combines historical and fantastical elements with clockwork automatons and shadowy conspiracies.

The Leviathan trilogy by Scott Westerfeld

Beginning with "Leviathan," this series offers an alternate history of World War I where the Central Powers use steam-powered machines and the Allies have genetically engineered creatures for warfare.

The Difference Engine by William Gibson and Bruce Sterling

This novel is often considered one of the early works that helped define the steampunk genre, presenting an alternative history where steam-powered computers shape society.

The Peculiar by Stefan Bachmann

This novel is set in an alternate Victorian London where faeries and clockwork creatures exist, and it follows the adventures of a young boy with unusual abilities.

The Alchemy of Stone by Ekaterina Sedia

This novel is set in a city powered by alchemy and machinery, featuring a sentient automaton named Mattie who becomes embroiled in a political struggle that could change the city's future. It explores themes of artificial intelligence, social unrest, and the clash between magic and technology in a steampunk world.

Fairy-tale Retelling Fantasy

Fairy-tale retelling fantasy is a subgenre of literature that reimagines and reinterprets classic fairy tales, myths, or folklore, often placing them in new settings, adding twists to the familiar plots, and giving depth to iconic characters. These retellings may range from faithful adaptations to entirely inventive reimaginings, and they often explore themes like the power of storytelling, subverting traditional roles, and the enduring relevance of these timeless tales in contemporary contexts.

These titles showcase the creativity and variety within the fairy-tale retelling fantasy subgenre, offering fresh perspectives on beloved stories and characters:

Cinder by Marissa Meyer

This book is the first in the "Lunar Chronicles" series, reimagining Cinderella as a talented mechanic in a futuristic world.

The Bear and the Nightingale by Katherine Arden

Drawing from Russian folklore, this novel retells the story of Vasilisa the Beautiful, who can see spirits and must confront supernatural forces.

Queen of Snow by Laura Byron and Jessie Cal

This series reimagines Snow White as a fierce warrior, exploring her journey in a dark and complex fantasy world.

Uprooted by Naomi Novik

This standalone novel offers a fresh take on the Beauty and the Beast story, set in a village near a mysterious, malevolent forest.

A Court of Thorns and Roses by Sarah J. Maas

This series reimagines Beauty and the Beast in a world of faeries, blending romance and adventure in a richly developed fantasy realm.

Spinning Silver by Naomi Novik

Inspired by Rumpelstiltskin, this novel weaves a tale of clever women, icy fey lords, and the power of words in a wintry fantasy setting.
